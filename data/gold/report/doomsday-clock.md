# Doomsday Clock

The Doomsday Clock is a symbolic timepiece maintained by the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists that represents humanity's proximity to catastrophic destruction. Rather than marking actual time, the clock functions as a metaphorical warning system, with midnight representing the point of global catastrophe. The distance from midnight—measured in minutes and seconds—reflects the organization's assessment of how close civilization stands to existential threats.

Since its inauguration in June 1947, the Clock has become a universally recognized emblem of humanity's peril from unchecked scientific and technological advancement. The Bulletin's Science and Security Board evaluates developments in nuclear weapons, climate change, artificial intelligence, and other potential threats to human survival each January, adjusting the Clock accordingly. The original 1947 setting placed humanity at seven minutes to midnight. As of January 2026, the Clock stands at its closest point ever: 85 seconds to midnight.

## Origins and Purpose

The Doomsday Clock emerged from the work of the Chicago Atomic Scientists, researchers who had participated in the Manhattan Project. Following the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, these scientists began publishing the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists to educate the public about nuclear dangers. In 1947, co-founder Hyman Goldsmith commissioned artist Martyl Langsdorf to design a magazine cover featuring the Clock. According to co-founder Eugene Rabinowitch, the Clock was "not a gauge to register the ups and downs of the international power struggle; it is intended to reflect basic changes in the level of continuous danger in which mankind lives in the nuclear age."

Langsdorf selected clock imagery to convey urgency—the countdown suggests destruction will occur naturally unless deliberate action prevents it. The visual metaphor proved remarkably durable; the Clock has appeared on every cover of the Bulletin since its debut and has become the organization's defining symbol.

## Factors Influencing the Clock

The determination of when "midnight" occurs and how close the world stands to it involves complex deliberation by the Bulletin's board members. They consider multiple dimensions of global threat, including nuclear weapons, climate science, diplomacy, energy policy, and weapons development. Biological threats, bioterrorism, artificial intelligence, and information warfare have been added to the assessment framework over time.

The board members judge proximity to midnight by discussing humanity's distance from civilizational collapse. This subjective evaluation process has drawn criticism from those who question the methodology's rigor and objectivity. Nevertheless, the Clock has shifted 27 times since its inception, sometimes moving backward when geopolitical tensions ease and international cooperation increases, and forward when threats intensify.

## Historical Fluctuations

The Clock's journey over eight decades reveals the changing nature of global risks. In 1953, after both the United States and Soviet Union conducted hydrogen bomb tests, the Clock reached two minutes to midnight—matching the danger level finally exceeded only in 2020. The farthest point from midnight came in 1991, when the Clock retreated to 17 minutes following the end of the Cold War, the signing of the START I treaty, and the Soviet Union's dissolution.

The period from 1960 through 1972 witnessed significant movements toward safety as international scientific cooperation expanded and major arms control agreements were signed, including the Partial Test Ban Treaty and the Strategic Arms Limitation Talks treaties. However, subsequent decades brought renewed concern. The 1980s saw the Clock advance dramatically as tensions escalated under President Ronald Reagan, with U.S. and Soviet missiles deployed across Europe and rhetoric about winning the Cold War intensifying the arms race.

The post-Cold War optimism proved temporary. By the late 1990s and 2000s, concerns about nuclear proliferation, terrorism, and emerging climate science moved the Clock forward again. In 2007, climate change was formally added as a major threat equivalent to nuclear weapons, marking a conceptual expansion beyond the original nuclear focus.

## Recent Developments and Criticism

In January 2018, the Clock moved to two minutes to midnight, cited as the third-closest approach since inception. Scientists pointed to failed international leadership on nuclear weapons and climate change, along with provocative rhetoric and actions from North Korea and the Trump administration. The Clock remained at two minutes in 2019 despite expectations of further movement, with board members emphasizing how information warfare and democracy's erosion amplified existing threats.

The Clock has drawn substantial criticism from multiple quarters. Cognitive psychologist Steven Pinker has characterized it as a political stunt divorced from objective security indicators, noting that it showed greater danger in 2007 than during the Cuban Missile Crisis—a comparison he finds absurd. Others argue the "grab bag of threats" combined on the Clock creates paralyzing anxiety rather than motivating constructive policy changes.

Conservative critics have accused the Bulletin of pursuing a political agenda, overestimating threats from climate change, and conflating diverse risks inappropriately. Some observers question whether the Clock's methodology can withstand scrutiny or whether it merely reflects the board's subjective anxieties about contemporary politics.

Despite these criticisms, the Clock continues to command significant attention. According to the Bulletin, the Doomsday Clock attracts more daily website visitors than any other feature, demonstrating its enduring power as a cultural symbol communicating urgency about humanity's existential challenges.
