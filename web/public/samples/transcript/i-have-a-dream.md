# I Have a Dream

"I Have a Dream" is a public speech delivered by American civil rights activist and Baptist minister Martin Luther King Jr. during the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom on August 28, 1963. In the speech, King called for civil and economic rights and an end to legalized racism in the United States.

Delivered to over 250,000 civil rights supporters from the steps of the Lincoln Memorial in Washington, D.C., the speech became one of the most famous moments of the civil rights movement and among the most iconic speeches in American history.

Beginning with a reference to the Emancipation Proclamation, King stated that "one hundred years later, the Negro still is not free." Toward the end of the speech, King departed from his prepared text for an improvised conclusion on the theme "I have a dream." Singer Mahalia Jackson, seated behind him, encouraged King by shouting encouragement just before he began his most famous segment.

The speech was ranked the top American speech of the 20th century in a 1999 poll of scholars of public address.

## Background

The March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom was partly intended to demonstrate mass support for civil rights legislation proposed by President John F. Kennedy in June 1963. Martin Luther King Jr. and other leaders agreed to keep their speeches calm to avoid provoking the civil disobedience that had characterized the movement. King originally designed his speech as a homage to Abraham Lincoln's Gettysburg Address, timed to correspond with the centennial of Lincoln's Emancipation Proclamation.

King had been preaching about dreams since 1960, when he delivered a speech to the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People called "The Negro and the American Dream." In 1961, King incorporated "the dream" as the centerpiece for several national speeches about the civil rights movement and student activists' aspirations for equality. On November 27, 1962, King gave a speech at Booker T. Washington High School in Rocky Mount, North Carolina that contained identical portions, including the "I have a dream" refrain. King also delivered a speech with the "I have a dream" refrain in Detroit in June 1963, speaking to 25,000 people following the Great Walk to Freedom march.

The March on Washington speech was drafted with assistance from Stanley Levison and Clarence Benjamin Jones in Riverdale, New York City. The speech went through several versions and has no single draft, but rather represents an amalgamation of several drafts. It was originally called "Normalcy, Never Again." The focus on "I have a dream" emerged through the speech's delivery, as King departed from his prepared remarks near the end to preach improvisationally.

## Speech

The speech draws upon appeals to America's myths as a nation founded to provide freedom and justice to all people, then reinforces and transcends those secular mythologies by placing them within a spiritual context. King describes the promises made by America as a "promissory note" on which America has defaulted, saying that "America has given the Negro people a bad check," but that the marchers had come to cash this check.

Widely hailed as a masterpiece of rhetoric, King's speech alludes to pivotal documents in American history, including the Emancipation Proclamation, the Declaration of Independence, and the Constitution. Early in the speech, he alludes to the Gettysburg Address by saying "Five score years ago." The speech, infused with the cadences of a sermon, contains allusions to biblical verses including Isaiah 40:4-5 and Amos 5:24.

The most widely cited example of anaphora appears in the "I have a dream" refrain, which is repeated eight times as King paints a picture of an integrated America. Other repeated phrases include "One hundred years later," "We can never be satisfied," "With this faith," "Let freedom ring," and "free at last."

Among the most quoted lines: "I have a dream that my four little children will one day live in a nation where they will not be judged by the color of their skin but by the content of their character."

## Responses

The speech was lauded in the days after the event and was widely considered the high point of the March by contemporary observers. Reporter James Reston wrote for The New York Times that King "was full of the symbolism of Lincoln and Gandhi, and the cadences of the Bible. He was both militant and sad."

An article in The Boston Globe reported that King's speech "caught the mood" and "moved the crowd" as no other speaker had. Marquis Childs of The Washington Post wrote that King's speech "rose above mere oratory."

The Federal Bureau of Investigation, which viewed King and his allies as subversive, noticed the speech and expanded their COINTELPRO operation against the Southern Christian Leadership Conference. Two days after the speech, FBI agent William C. Sullivan wrote a memo describing King as "the most dangerous Negro of the future in this Nation."

The speech was a success for the Kennedy administration and for the liberal civil rights coalition that had planned it. It was considered a "triumph of managed protest," and no arrests relating to the demonstration occurred. President Kennedy, who watched the speech on television, was very impressed and felt the march bolstered chances for his civil rights bill.

Some Black leaders later criticized the speech as too compromising. Malcolm X questioned "angry revolutionaries swinging their bare feet together with their oppressor."
