# 2024 Summer Olympics

The 2024 Summer Olympics, officially known as the Games of the XXXIII Olympiad and branded as Paris 2024, took place in France from July 26 to August 11, 2024, with certain events beginning on July 24. Paris served as the host city, with additional events distributed across 16 other metropolitan French locations. Football matches occurred in cities including Marseille, Lyon, Bordeaux, Nantes, Nice, and Saint-Étienne. A notable exception was the surfing competition, held in Teahupo'o, Tahiti, in French Polynesia, approximately 15,716 kilometers from Paris.

This marked Paris's third hosting of the Summer Olympics, following 1900 and 1924. The city thus became only the second location ever to host the Summer Games three times, with London being the first. The 2024 Games commemorated the centennial of Paris's 1924 Olympics and the inaugural 1924 Winter Olympics in Chamonix. France hosted the Summer Games for the third time and Winter Games three times across its Olympic history.

The Games featured 10,714 athletes competing across 329 events in 32 sports comprising 48 disciplines. Breaking made its Olympic debut, while skateboarding, sport climbing, and surfing returned after debuting in 2020. The event was the first to achieve full gender parity in athlete participation.

The opening ceremony, directed by Thomas Jolly, departed from tradition by occurring outside a stadium. Instead, athletes paraded by boat along the Seine from Pont d'Austerlitz to Pont d'Iéna, with cultural performances at various landmarks. The flame was lit by judoka Teddy Riner and sprinter Marie-José Pérec. For the first time, the Olympic cauldron used LED lighting and aerosol jets rather than actual combustion.

The United States topped the medal count with 40 gold and 126 total medals, marking its fourth consecutive Summer Olympics at the top position. China tied the U.S. on gold medals (40) but finished second with 91 total medals—the first time these two most successful Olympic nations tied in gold. Japan earned 20 gold and 45 total medals, while Australia achieved 18 gold and 53 total, marking its most successful Summer Olympics. France, the host nation, secured 16 gold and 64 total medals, their best finish since 1996. Several nations won their first-ever Olympic medals, including Dominica and Saint Lucia (each earning gold), Cape Verde, and Albania.

The Games recorded over 9.5 million ticket sales out of 10 million available, surpassing the previous record from the 1996 Atlanta Olympics. Despite controversies regarding logistics, politics, and Olympic Village conditions, observers and Parisians largely considered the Games successful.

## Background

Paris did not pursue hosting the Olympics again until bidding for 2024, which would mark the centenary of its 1924 Games. Previous unsuccessful bids targeted 1992, 2008, and 2012. Six cities initially entered the bidding process: Paris, Hamburg, Boston, Budapest, Rome, and Los Angeles. The competition faced significant obstacles from withdrawals and rising costs.

Boston and the United States Olympic Committee mutually terminated Boston's bid on July 27, 2015, citing insufficient public support. Hamburg withdrew after holding a referendum on November 29, 2015. Rome withdrew on September 21, 2016, citing financial constraints. Budapest withdrew on February 22, 2017, following a petition that gathered sufficient signatures to trigger a referendum.

After these withdrawals, the IOC Executive Board met on June 9, 2017, in Lausanne, Switzerland, to address the 2024 and 2028 selection processes. The International Olympic Committee formally proposed electing both years' host cities simultaneously. An Extraordinary IOC Session approved this proposal on July 11, 2017.

The IOC subsequently established procedures for Los Angeles 2024 and Paris 2024 bid committees to negotiate which city would host which Olympiad. Following negotiations, the IOC announced Los Angeles as sole candidate for 2028 on July 31, 2017, enabling Paris confirmation for 2024. Both bids received praise for their technical plans and innovative use of existing and temporary facilities. Both decisions were ratified at the 131st IOC Session on September 13, 2017.

Paris was elected as host on September 13, 2017, at the 131st IOC Session in Lima, Peru. The two French IOC members were ineligible to vote under Olympic Charter rules.

## Venues

Most Olympic events took place in Paris and its metropolitan region, including neighboring cities such as Saint-Denis, Le Bourget, Nanterre, Versailles, and Vaires-sur-Marne. Beyond this core zone, basketball preliminaries and handball finals occurred in Lille, 225 kilometers away. Marseille, 777 kilometers distant, hosted sailing and some football matches. Teahupo'o in Tahiti hosted surfing at a distance of 15,716 kilometers.

The Paris Centre zone encompassed multiple iconic venues. Parc des Princes hosted football matches with a capacity of 48,583. Stade Roland Garros accommodated tennis, boxing finals, and other events across three courts (15,000, 12,000, and 9,000 capacity). Paris Expo Porte de Versailles hosted volleyball, table tennis, handball preliminaries, and weightlifting with combined capacity of 18,000 across two arenas. The Accor Arena (Bercy) held artistic and trampolining gymnastics plus basketball finals with 15,000 capacity. The Grand Palais hosted fencing and taekwondo with 8,000 seats, while Place de la Concorde accommodated 3x3 basketball, breaking, and BMX freestyle on a temporary installation.

The Eiffel Tower Stadium at Champ de Mars hosted beach volleyball with 12,000 capacity. Les Invalides featured archery, the marathon finish, and cycling time trial start points. The Jardins du Trocadéro hosted the opening ceremony for 30,000 spectators, with 13,000 seated and the remainder standing. The Seine itself served as the opening ceremony route, with capacity for approximately 300,000 viewers.

The Grand Paris zone contained the Stade de France with 77,083 capacity for rugby sevens, athletics, and the closing ceremony. The Paris Aquatic Centre held water polo preliminaries, diving, and artistic swimming with 5,000 capacity. The Porte de La Chapelle Arena, newly built for the Games, accommodated badminton and rhythmic gymnastics with 8,000 seats.

The Versailles zone featured the Palace of Versailles gardens with 80,000 total capacity split between equestrian events and modern pentathlon. Le Golf National held golf competitions with 35,000 capacity. Élancourt Hill hosted mountain biking with 25,000 spectators. The Vélodrome de Saint-Quentin-en-Yvelines accommodated track cycling and BMX racing with 5,000 capacity each.
